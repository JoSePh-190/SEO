Press Bio

Who is Gbarabe Tordi Joseph?

Engineer. Developer. Builder of tools that bridge the gap between petroleum science and the modern web.

Gbarabe Tordi Joseph is a final-year Petroleum Engineering student at the University of Port Harcourt — Nigeria's foremost institution for petroleum and energy engineering — and a professional software developer with a specialisation in Python and the Django web framework.

He occupies a rare position in the Nigerian technical landscape: someone who has trained rigorously in the subsurface sciences and can simultaneously build the software to automate, communicate, and scale the work those sciences demand. Where most engineers reach for spreadsheets and most developers reach for generic business applications, Gbarabe builds purpose-built tools — web applications that encode real engineering knowledge and put it directly in the hands of practitioners and students.

His undergraduate programme at UNIPORT has taken him through the full arc of hydrocarbon production: reservoir fluid behaviour, petrophysics, material balance analysis, enhanced oil recovery, well completion, production optimisation, and petroleum economics. His final-year research project applies computational methods to a live reservoir engineering problem — a fitting synthesis of the two disciplines he has spent years developing in parallel.

The Developer

Software Built for Engineering Problems

Gbarabe began writing software as a direct response to a frustration he experienced early in his engineering studies: the tools available to students were either prohibitively expensive, locked behind institutional licences, or so general-purpose that they offered no real help with the specific calculations petroleum engineers actually perform. He set out to change that.

Working in Python — the dominant language of scientific computing — and Django, the battle-tested web framework that powers some of the world's most demanding applications, he has built a portfolio of engineering web applications that are accessible, accurate, and free to use in a browser without installation.

Flagship Project

Material Balance Equation (MBE) Calculator

The MBE Calculator is Gbarabe's most technically ambitious project. The Material Balance Equation — first formalised by Havlena and Odeh in the 1960s — is the foundational tool of reservoir engineering, used to estimate original oil and gas in place and to diagnose the dominant drive mechanism sustaining a reservoir's production. In practice, solving it requires iterating over production history, fluid PVT data, and compressibility terms in ways that are tedious and error-prone when done manually.

Gbarabe's solver accepts production and fluid data, performs the numerical analysis, and returns graphical diagnostics — Havlena-Odeh straight-line plots and drive-index breakdowns — rendered directly in the browser. What once required a commercial simulator or a carefully constructed spreadsheet can now be completed in minutes by any engineer with an internet connection.

Applied EOR

Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) Calculator

Enhanced Oil Recovery is one of the most consequential and technically complex areas of petroleum engineering — determining which recovery method is suitable for a given reservoir requires screening dozens of technical parameters against established criteria for water flooding, polymer flooding, CO₂ injection, steam injection, and in-situ combustion. Gbarabe's EOR Calculator automates this screening process.

The tool evaluates a user-supplied reservoir description against published technical screening guidelines, ranks candidate EOR methods by suitability, and generates incremental recovery estimates — producing, in a few seconds, the kind of preliminary screening report that would otherwise take an engineer several hours to compile from technical literature.

Platform

Engineering Web Applications Suite

Beyond his flagship calculators, Gbarabe has developed a broader suite of Django-powered engineering tools covering workflows from Darcy's Law permeability estimation to wellbore pressure loss and nodal analysis. Each application is built on the same principle: encode the engineering correctly, then build an interface that makes it impossible to use incorrectly.

The suite reflects his belief that educational software should not just give answers — it should show its working, explain its assumptions, and help the user develop an intuition for the underlying physics. Every tool he ships includes result explanations alongside the calculated output.

The Engineer

Trained at the Heart of Nigeria's Oil Sector

The University of Port Harcourt sits in Rivers State — the centre of Nigeria's oil and gas industry and one of the most significant petroleum provinces in sub-Saharan Africa. Studying petroleum engineering here means studying it in close proximity to the actual fields, the actual companies, and the actual engineering challenges the discipline exists to solve.

That context has shaped Gbarabe's engineering perspective profoundly. His interest is not abstract: he understands that the calculations his software performs are the same calculations that inform billion-dollar investment decisions in fields that supply energy to millions of people. That weight of consequence is something he carries into both his engineering work and his software — correctness is not optional, and the engineering always comes first.
